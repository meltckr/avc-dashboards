GAME SUMMARY
Short-handed Suns overwhelmed by defending champs
▶
⚠ Key Absences
OUT — Devin Booker (ankle), Jalen Green (hamstring), Grayson Allen (knee). Ott elected to rest Booker and Green on the second night of a back-to-back, prioritizing health heading into the All-Star break.
The Suns never had a chance. Oklahoma City — even without Shai Gilgeous-Alexander — jumped out to a 37-25 first-quarter lead behind 9 early points from Luguentz Dort and never looked back. The Thunder shot 11-of-18 (61.1%) from three in the first half alone, building a 75-52 halftime advantage. Jalen Williams was virtually unstoppable, going 11-of-11 from the field before his only miss, scoring 19 of his 28 points in the third quarter and pushing OKC's lead to 37. Seven Thunder players scored in double figures. Dillon Brooks led Phoenix with 23 points but picked up his 16th technical foul of the season, triggering an automatic one-game suspension after the All-Star break. The fourth quarter was garbage time, allowing rookies Rasheer Fleming, Khaman Maluach, and Koby Brea to log developmental minutes.

KEY PERFORMERS — SUNS
Brooks leads scoring; Bouyea steps up with 11 & 6
▶
Dillon Brooks (23 pts, 5 reb) — Led the team in scoring for the second consecutive night. Carried the offensive load without Booker and Green but also picked up his 16th technical foul, earning an automatic one-game suspension. His competitive fire is a double-edged sword.

Royce O'Neale (12 pts) — Continued his reliable floor-spacing role. Hit threes to keep Phoenix within shouting distance in the first half when it mattered.

Jordan Goodwin (12 pts, 4 reb) — Solid effort on both ends. One of the few Suns who competed hard from wire to wire despite the deficit.

Jamaree Bouyea (11 pts, 6 ast) — Stepped into a starting role with Booker and Green out and delivered the best assist total on the team. Showed he can run an offense in emergency duty — a useful data point for the front office.

Mark Williams — Contributed on the interior with dunks and alley-oops (6 pts in Q1) but couldn't match OKC's length, particularly Holmgren and Jaylin Williams, on the glass.

Amir Coffey (second game) — In his second game since arriving at the deadline, Coffey continued adjusting to the system. Limited role in a blowout context but got additional reps.

Rookies: Fleming, Maluach, Brea — All received extended garbage-time minutes in the fourth quarter. Developmental reps that matter for long-term roster evaluation.

DILLON BROOKS | F — 16TH TECHNICAL FOUL
23 PTS • 5 REB • Season-high 16 techs → 1-game suspension
▶
⚠ Suspension Alert
Brooks' 16th technical triggers an automatic one-game suspension following the All-Star break. He will miss the Feb. 19 game at San Antonio unless the league rescinds the tech. Additional fine of $5,000. Every two techs after 16 results in another one-game suspension.
Brooks was assessed his 16th technical in the second quarter after arguing with official James Williams on a foul call against Thunder guard Jared McCain with the Suns down 18. Brooks says the official told him he was "playing the victim too much." He leads the league in technical fouls this season.
"If you're going to be consistently bad, then be consistently bad throughout the whole game. Don't try to fix it during the game. Don't try to be doing any of that, or try to even out foul calls or whatever it may be."
On officiating consistency postgame
📌 Ownership Note: Brooks' competitive fire is a feature, not a bug — his 21.2 ppg on career-high shooting (44.1% FG) and 17.6 FGA per game reflect his importance to the offense. But 16 techs is a real cost: $5K fine plus the suspension. The risk compounds if he picks up two more (another suspension). Worth a private conversation about channeling intensity without crossing the line, especially in playoff-critical stretches. Ott's public defense of Brooks ("everyone was frustrated") is the right messaging to keep the player bought in.

OKLAHOMA CITY CONTEXT
Defending champions • 42-13 • Won 4 of 6 • Also missing SGA
▶
Oklahoma City dominated despite missing Shai Gilgeous-Alexander (abdominal strain, fourth straight game out) and Ajay Mitchell (abdominal strain). SGA — second in the league in scoring at 31.8 ppg — was not available, yet the Thunder's depth was absurd. Seven players scored in double figures. The bench produced 74 points.
Jalen Williams was the headliner, scoring 28 points on 11-of-12 shooting — he didn't miss any of his first 11 shots. He poured in 19 of his 28 in the third quarter before checking himself out holding his hamstring. Isaiah Joe caught fire with 21 points on 6-of-8 from three — his third straight high-scoring game since the trade deadline, stepping up in SGA's absence. Kenrich Williams added 15 (6 rebounds, 4 offensive) and Jared McCain, in his most extensive action in a Thunder uniform, had 12 points with 2 steals in 22 minutes.
Jaylin Williams (11 pts, 6 reb, 5 ast, 3 blk) was the statistical Swiss Army knife, contributing to every category. Chet Holmgren (13 pts, 5-5 FT) was efficient without needing to dominate.
📌 Ownership Note: This game confirmed what the league already knows — OKC's depth is historically elite. They scored 136 without their best player. The Jalen Williams hamstring concern (checked himself out in Q3, only his second game back from a 10-game absence) is worth monitoring: if Williams misses time again, it changes OKC's ceiling and Phoenix's potential playoff path. Isaiah Joe's post-deadline surge (three consecutive high-scoring games) signals the Thunder's trade deadline acquisitions are already producing — a warning sign for the West.

OWNERSHIP THEMES
Signals for Mat Ishbia / front office
▶
1. The rest decision was correct. Ott's choice to sit Booker (ankle) and Green (hamstring) on the second night of a back-to-back before a week-long All-Star break was the right call. Both players were back from injuries just one night earlier. The 27-point loss is the cost of prioritizing March-June health over a February regular-season game. There is no universe in which risking re-injury to either player for this game makes strategic sense.
2. Brooks' tech situation requires internal management. 16 technicals and a one-game suspension is a leadership issue that needs to be addressed privately. Brooks is averaging career highs across the board (21.2 ppg, 44.1% FG, 17.6 FGA) and his competitive intensity is a core part of his value — but the suspensions create real competitive cost. Missing the Spurs game on Feb. 19 is manageable; missing playoff games would be devastating. The front office and Ott should have a calibrated conversation: validate the fire, redirect the expression.
3. Depth passed the stress test — partially. Without three rotation guards (Booker, Green, Allen), the Suns scored 109 against the league's best team. Bouyea (11/6) showed he can function as an emergency point guard. Goodwin and O'Neale provided their usual reliability. The bad news: the drop-off from Booker/Green to Bouyea/Gillespie is massive, and OKC's bench (74 points, 7 double-figure scorers) exposed the gap. This is expected but confirms the Suns' ceiling is star-dependent.
4. OKC is a different animal — even without SGA. The Thunder scored 136 without their best player. Their 58.4% field goal shooting and 74 bench points represent a systemic advantage, not a one-off. If the Suns face OKC in the playoffs, the game plan must account for this depth. The Jalen Williams hamstring concern is the one vulnerability to monitor — if he misses significant time again, OKC's ceiling drops meaningfully.
5. All-Star break timing is a gift. The Suns enter the break at 32-23 — firmly in the playoff picture — with Booker headed to the 3-Point Contest and All-Star Game. The break provides recovery time for Booker's ankle, Green's hamstring, and Allen's knee. It also gives Amir Coffey time to absorb the playbook and Brooks time to decompress before the suspension game. The narrative heading into the break is: healthy and positioned, not desperate and crumbling.
6. Three losses in four games is a trend to monitor, not a crisis. The Suns lost to Golden State (101-97), Philadelphia (109-103), and now OKC (136-109) after beating Portland. The Warriors and Sixers losses were competitive; this one was a rest-game outlier. If the losing continues post-break, it becomes a genuine concern. For now, the underlying data (Booker back, Green back, Coffey added, depth performing) supports optimism.
